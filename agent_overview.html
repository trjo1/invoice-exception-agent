The P2P Exception Orchestrator

A walkthrough of what this agent does, the data it works with, the exceptions it handles, and where humans stay in the loop. Written for procurement and AP practice leads — non-technical, focused on scope.

1. The problem we're solving

Procure-to-pay exceptions are the part of AP that doesn't fit on a clerk's screen. The clean invoices — 3-way match passes, supplier known, terms right, GR posted — flow through. The exceptions don't. A typical mid-market enterprise sees 1–3% of invoices fall out as exceptions, each taking 10–20 minutes of clerk time across SAP, Ariba, ServiceNow, supplier email, and the buyer's procurement liaison. At scale that's full-time roles spent on triage rather than sourcing or supplier strategy.

What makes exceptions hard isn't the volume — it's that each one needs context from multiple systems. A duplicate-invoice catch requires the invoice plus the supplier-invoice-number history plus payment status. A price variance requires the invoice plus the PO master plus the goods receipt. A fraud signal requires the invoice plus the vendor master plus the last 30 days of vendor changes. Most AP tools handle one of these well; none handle all of them, and none decide what to do next.

Agent 1 sits in that gap. It watches the exceptions, looks up the context, classifies what's wrong, recommends the right next step with rationale, drafts any communication, and routes to the right human for sign-off. The clerk goes from "do triage" to "approve the agent's call."

2. What the agent is, in one paragraph

The agent receives one invoice at a time (eventually from SAP / Ariba / email; today via upload or a curated sample picker). It reads the PDF, pulls structured fields out of it, looks up everything the buyer's master data has on the supplier and the related PO and goods receipt, decides what category of exception this is (one of 13), retrieves the most relevant policies from the buyer's policy library, picks a recommended next action with rationale and a counterfactual ("if X were different the recommendation would be Y"), routes it to the right human tier, drafts any supplier or internal communication, and once approved, writes the outcome back to SAP / Ariba / ServiceNow. Everything is logged in an audit trail you can replay months later.

4. What happens at each step

Plain-English walkthrough of the ten nodes.

1Event ingestion

An invoice arrives. Today this is a PDF the user uploads. In production it'll be a SAP webhook firing on every new invoice, a polling job that walks Ariba's open exceptions, a watcher on the AP shared inbox for supplier-emailed invoices, or a ServiceNow trigger when a discrepancy ticket is opened. The agent normalizes them all into one common shape so the rest of the pipeline doesn't care where the invoice came from.

2Extract

The agent reads the PDF and pulls out the structured fields: supplier name and tax ID, PO reference, invoice number and date, currency, payment terms, line items with quantity / unit price / line total, tax breakdown, and grand total. It extracts verbatim — typos and odd formatting stay, the agent doesn't quietly "correct" the supplier's invoice. Correction is what the next steps decide.

3Cross-case context

The agent now looks the invoice up against six master-data sources: vendor master (is the supplier known?), PO master (what did we authorize?), goods receipts (did we actually receive it?), payment status (have we paid this PO already?), invoice history (have we seen this exact invoice before? Other invoices on this PO?), and vendor changes (did the supplier's bank account or address change recently?). The output is a "case in context" — everything we know about this invoice and its surroundings, in one bundle.

4Classify

With the invoice in hand and the context bundle ready, the agent picks one of 13 exception categories — or "no exception" if it's clean. The classifier doesn't just look at the invoice in isolation; it weighs the smart signals from the context (duplicate invoice number on the same PO, vendor not in master, PO already paid in full, etc.) and assigns the category that best fits. Section 6 of this doc lists all 13 categories with examples.

5Retrieve policies

The agent searches the buyer's policy library — the rules document covering tolerances, escalation thresholds, tax rules, fraud triggers, FX clauses, etc. — and pulls back the 5 most relevant policy snippets for this case. These are what the next step will cite when it recommends an action. The agent doesn't make up rules out of thin air; it grounds its recommendation in the buyer's actual policy.

6Decide

This is the agent's "what should we do?" step. It picks one of 16 specific recommended actions (request supplier credit memo, ask for the missing PO, escalate to fraud, halt pay run, route to VP finance, etc.) and produces a rationale that cites the specific facts of this case ("PO authorized $25/unit; invoice billed $27/unit — 8% above the 2% tolerance band in policy 001"), plus a counterfactual — one sentence stating what would have to be different for the recommendation to change. The counterfactual is what makes the recommendation auditable to a human reviewer.

7Route

The agent decides which human (if any) needs to approve before the recommended action fires. Rules-based, deterministic — same recommendation maps to the same tier and the same named role every time. Tier 1 means auto-pass (no human review); Tier 2 means the AP buyer team or a specific named team (vendor master, procurement) approves; Tier 3 means a supervisor — fraud team, treasury, VP finance — has to sign off. Section 7 of this doc explains the tiers.

8Draft

If the recommended action involves communicating with the supplier (e.g. "request a credit memo for the over-billed amount") or with an internal team ("notify procurement of the supplier delay" or "escalate this duplicate to the fraud team"), the agent drafts the actual email or note. Professional, neutral, references the specific facts of the case. Never auto-sent — the reviewer in the next step approves, edits-and-approves, or rejects before it goes anywhere. Tier 1 cases skip this step entirely.

9Human approval

The case lands in a review queue. The reviewer sees the agent's classification, the recommendation with rationale and counterfactual, the cited policies, the draft communication, and the underlying data (invoice + PO + GR + vendor master + smart signals). Three actions: Approve the agent's call as-is, Edit and approve (modify the draft before sending), or Reject (the agent got it wrong; case closes with a note). Every action goes to the audit log.

10Execute

Once a human has approved (or for Tier 1, immediately), the agent fires the actual downstream side effects: post the invoice to SAP, send the supplier email, open a ServiceNow ticket for procurement, halt the pay run, page the fraud team, log the audit record. Each action is a specific recipe of one to three downstream calls — the agent doesn't improvise the integration side. If a reviewer edited the draft before approving, the executor uses the edited version.

5. The data the agent consults

The agent looks at six master-data sources, all of which the buyer's procurement / AP organization already maintains. In production these are SAP S/4HANA Cloud (or Microsoft Dynamics) API calls. In the current build they're populated from a representative test corpus that mirrors a real enterprise's master data shape and size.

Vendor master

The supplier's record: name, tax ID, country, addresses, contract type (MSA / spot / recurring), tier (strategic / tactical / new / inactive), status (active / blocked), sanctions check result, bank account fingerprint, onboarding date, special classifications (SBE/MBE/HIPAA).

PO master

The authorized purchase order: ID, vendor, line items with authorized quantity and unit price, total authorized, currency, payment terms, approver chain (who signed off on what and when), status (open / partially received / fully received / invoiced / paid / closed), department, emergency flag, FX clause.

Goods receipts

What the warehouse actually received: receipt ID, receipt date, warehouse, receiver, and per-line quantities received against each PO line. The third leg of three-way match.

Payment status

Aggregated state of the PO: total authorized, total invoiced so far, total paid so far, number of invoices booked, last payment date. Lets the agent see if a PO is already fully paid before accepting a new invoice against it.

Invoice history

Every prior invoice from this supplier, indexed two ways: by supplier invoice number (for duplicate detection) and by PO ID (for split-invoice fraud detection). The agent looks here before accepting a "new" invoice as actually new.

Vendor changes

Master-data edits in the last 30 days: bank account changes, address changes, tax ID changes, each with date and note. A bank-account change immediately before a high-value invoice is the classic vendor-impersonation pattern.

Beyond master data, the agent also consults a policy library — the buyer's written rules covering tolerances (e.g., "price variance within 2% auto-resolves"), escalation thresholds (e.g., "spend over $50K needs VP finance"), tax rules per jurisdiction, fraud triggers, FX clauses, and special-case carve-outs (e.g., "strategic vendors get 5% tolerance band"). The library is the place where buyer-specific business logic lives — different buyers have different policies, and the agent reads each buyer's library at runtime rather than having any rules hard-coded into the model.

6. The 13 exception scenarios it handles

Every invoice gets one of these 13 labels. Each label has a typical recommended action and a routing tier (the per-case tier can still escalate based on dollar exposure or confidence). The colors in the cards reflect the routing tier — green = auto-pass, amber = buyer / team review, red = supervisor / fraud / treasury.

Clean — no exception

Invoice matches its PO and goods receipt cleanly. Supplier in good standing, currency and payment terms aligned, totals within tolerance. The agent auto-passes these.

Tier 1 · auto-pass
Price variance vs. PO

Invoiced unit price differs from PO-authorized price beyond the tolerance band. Either a supplier over-charge or a missed PO amendment. Agent asks supplier for credit memo or correction.

Tier 2 · buyer
Quantity variance vs. GR

Invoice claims a quantity that doesn't match the goods receipt (short or over-delivery). Same unit price, wrong count. Buyer confirms what actually arrived.

Tier 2 · buyer
PO reference missing

Invoice has no PO number, or references a PO that doesn't exist in the master. Common for emergency / retroactive PO scenarios. Buyer decides retroactive PO vs. reject.

Tier 2 · buyer
Goods receipt not yet posted

Invoice arrived but the warehouse hasn't confirmed receipt. Hold and re-check on the next polling cycle — don't pay against an unreceived shipment.

Tier 2 · buyer
Authorization gap

Spend exceeds the PO's authorized total, or capex routed to an opex approver. Needs a higher signing tier before posting.

Tier 3 · VP finance
Duplicate invoice

Same supplier invoice number already booked on this PO, or the PO is already paid in full. Smart filter requires same number AND matching total before firing — avoids false positives.

Tier 2 · buyer
Fraud signal

Vendor bank account just changed and the invoice is high-value, split-invoicing pattern detected, or sanctions hit (OFAC SDN). Pay run halts and the AP fraud team is paged.

Tier 3 · fraud team
Vendor not in master

Supplier's tax ID and name don't match any active vendor record. Could be onboarding lag or a fictitious supplier — vendor master team investigates before payment.

Tier 2 · vendor master team
Currency mismatch

PO authorized in one currency, invoice billed in another. Either legitimate per the FX clause, or an FX-rate dispute requiring treasury sign-off.

Tier 2 · buyer / treasury
Tax field anomaly

Tax rate, jurisdiction, or amount on the invoice doesn't match what the buyer's tax rules expect (EU VAT on a US-only supplier; missing IGST on an Indian invoice; wrong rate).

Tier 2 · buyer
Payment terms mismatch

Invoice asserts NET-15 when the PO/MSA specifies NET-30 (or vice versa). Catches suppliers trying to accelerate payment terms without a contract change.

Tier 2 · buyer
Other / non-standard

Catch-all for exceptions that don't fit the 12 named categories. Defaults to buyer review with the agent's rationale and suggested next action.

Tier 2 · buyer

Each scenario maps to one or two of 16 recommended actions. The recommendation is the "what to do", the routing tier is "who decides". Decoupling them is what lets the same exception escalate to fraud / treasury / VP when the dollar exposure or confidence warrants it, without rewriting the classifier each time.

7. How humans stay in control

The agent never moves money or sends external communication on its own except in the cleanest auto-pass cases — and even those have guardrails (both the classifier and the recommender have to be highly confident, otherwise the case downgrades to Tier 2 review). Three review tiers cover everything else.

Tier 1

Auto-pass

Clean cases the agent posts to SAP without a human touching them. Used only when classification is "no exception" with high confidence AND the recommended action is auto-resolve with high confidence. Anything below the bar downgrades automatically.

Tier 2

Buyer / team approval

The default for most exception types. The buyer's AP team approves; specific cases route to the vendor master team (onboarding) or procurement (PO amendments). Reviewer can approve, edit-and-approve (modify the supplier email before send), or reject.

Tier 3

Supervisor / fraud / treasury / VP

Reserved for fraud signals (fraud team + halt pay run), FX variance (treasury), spend over PO authorization (VP finance), and supervisor-required halts. Money and supplier communication wait for the named role's sign-off.

Every state change — agent recommendation, human approval, draft edit, rejection, execution — writes an immutable row to an audit log with timestamp, actor, action, and note. The original agent draft and the human-edited version are both kept. A compliance team six months from now can replay every decision and see exactly what the agent said, what the reviewer changed, and what got sent.

8. What gets measured

The agent instruments itself end-to-end. Every model call, every routing decision, every human action, every execution gets logged with cost, latency, and outcome. From that the agent reports:

  • Auto-pass rate — what fraction of invoices the agent resolved without any human touch. The cleanest measure of "is the agent doing the work or are we?"
  • Classification mix — how often each of the 13 categories fires. Tells the practice lead whether the agent is biased (over- or under-firing on any category).
  • Human-resolution breakdown — approve vs. edit-and-approve vs. reject rates per action type. Edit-rate is the leading indicator that a particular draft template needs work; reject-rate is the leading indicator that a particular classification or routing rule is wrong.
  • Cost per case — total LLM and infrastructure cost per invoice processed. Today this runs under one cent per invoice on open-source models.
  • Latency — wall-clock time from upload to final disposition, with per-step breakdown.

These metrics live behind a dashboard that buyer's AP leadership reviews weekly. They're also the basis of the quarterly outcome reports — the recurring engagement that runs after the initial deployment ships, where the operator reviews the agent's behavior with the buyer and adjusts the policies and routing rules as the business evolves.

9. In scope / out of scope

The agent is deliberately scoped. The build pattern (Coordination + Routing + Decision-Support with a Drafting sub-component) is what makes it shippable in 8–12 weeks. Adjacent workflows are candidates for sibling agents — not extensions of this one.

In scope

  • Invoice exception detection and classification (the 13 categories)
  • Cross-system context lookup (vendor master, PO, GR, history, payment status, vendor changes)
  • Policy-grounded recommendations with rationale and counterfactual
  • Three-tier human-in-the-loop routing with edit + audit
  • Supplier email drafts and internal team notes (never auto-sent)
  • Write-back to SAP, Ariba, ServiceNow, supplier email, PagerDuty
  • Stage 9 measurement and recurring quarterly review

Out of scope (for this agent)

  • Supplier discovery / sourcing — different agent pattern
  • Price negotiation — out-of-band, buyer-led
  • Contract / MSA management — adjacent workflow
  • Audit and compliance findings — planned as Agent 2
  • Claims triage and adjudication — planned as Agent 3
  • OCR on scanned / image-only PDFs — text-layer extraction today
  • Multi-language drafts beyond English — English drafts today; multilingual is a later add-on
What's running on mock data today
The orchestrator, the 13-category classification, the decision step, the routing, the drafting, the queue, the audit log, and the measurement dashboard are all live and tested end-to-end. The six master-data sources are populated from a representative test corpus. The action executor is in "mock mode" — every downstream call is logged as "would have done X" but no real SAP or email API is hit. The real-backend swap is gated on the buyer's SAP credentials; the executor interface stays the same, so the swap is a backend change without any pipeline rework.

10. What we want feedback on

The most useful feedback for the procurement practice:

  1. Are the 13 categories the right ones? Anything we're missing? Anything that should be merged? Anything we're naming wrong?
  2. Is the tier-routing model right? Specifically: is the "Tier 3 → fraud / treasury / VP finance" split correct, or are there buyer-side roles we're missing? Should "vendor_master_team" be Tier 2 or Tier 3 in your experience?
  3. Are the recommended actions credible? 16 actions feels comprehensive on paper — does the list match what a real AP clerk would do in practice?
  4. What's the right minimum demo for a buyer conversation? Is showing extract + classify + decide + queue + execute enough, or do we need to walk through 3-4 specific scenarios end-to-end?
  5. Pilot candidates. Which procurement clients would benefit most from this and would tolerate the design-partner workflow (some manual data preparation, weekly check-ins, iterating on policies)?
